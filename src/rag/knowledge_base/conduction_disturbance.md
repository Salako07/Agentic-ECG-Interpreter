# Conduction Disturbance

## Definition
The conduction disturbance superclass (CD) covers delays or blocks in the
cardiac conduction system, including bundle branch blocks, fascicular blocks,
and atrioventricular (AV) blocks.

## Bundle branch blocks
Both types widen the QRS to 120 milliseconds or more.
- Right bundle branch block: an rSR' pattern in V1 with a wide slurred S wave
  in leads I and V6.
- Left bundle branch block: a broad, notched, or monophasic R wave in leads I,
  V5, and V6, with loss of the normal septal q waves.

## Fascicular blocks
- Left anterior fascicular block: marked left axis deviation with qR complexes
  in the lateral limb leads and a normal or slightly widened QRS.
- Left posterior fascicular block: right axis deviation with other causes
  excluded; less common.

## Atrioventricular blocks
- First-degree: PR interval greater than 200 milliseconds, every P conducted.
- Second-degree Mobitz I (Wenckebach): progressive PR lengthening until a beat
  is dropped.
- Second-degree Mobitz II: intermittent non-conducted P waves without
  progressive PR lengthening; higher risk of progression.
- Third-degree (complete): no relationship between P waves and QRS complexes;
  atria and ventricles depolarize independently.

## Interpretation notes
QRS widening is the hallmark of bundle branch block, while the AV blocks are
defined by the relationship between P waves and QRS complexes. Distinguishing
Mobitz II from Mobitz I matters because Mobitz II carries a greater risk of
progression to complete block.
