# Myocardial Infarction

## Definition
Myocardial infarction (superclass MI) reflects myocardial necrosis, producing
characteristic changes in the ST segment, T wave, and QRS complex. The ECG
localizes the affected territory by the leads in which changes appear.

## Lead territories and coronary supply
- Inferior wall: leads II, III, and aVF. Usually the right coronary artery.
- Anteroseptal and anterior wall: leads V1 through V4. Usually the left
  anterior descending artery.
- Lateral wall: leads I, aVL, V5, and V6. Usually the left circumflex artery.
- Posterior wall: no direct leads; suspected from reciprocal ST depression and
  tall R waves in V1 through V3.

## Characteristic findings
- ST-segment elevation in the leads facing an area of acute injury.
- Reciprocal ST depression in leads opposite the injury territory.
- Pathological Q waves, indicating established necrosis.
- T-wave inversion during evolution and resolution.

## Evolution over time
Acute infarction typically progresses from hyperacute (tall, peaked) T waves,
to ST elevation, to development of Q waves, and finally to T-wave inversion as
the infarct evolves. The stage informs acuity.

## Localization principle
Because each lead views the heart from a fixed direction, the distribution of
abnormal leads maps to an anatomical wall and its supplying artery. Inferior
and anterior territories are the most commonly identified on a standard 12-lead
tracing. This lead-to-territory mapping is the core of MI localization.
